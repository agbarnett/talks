# 2026 Essential Skills for Statistics Leadership in Consultancy and Research Symposium

*Title*: Will AI take my job?

*Abstract*: AI has led to massive job cuts in journalism and programming. Are we next?

The language models are getting better and are now widely used. Many researchers will soon routinely use them to design and analyse their experiments, especially those researchers with no access to human statistical help. There’s a risk that statistical jobs done by humans will be outsourced to robots. This could happen even if the robots are not better than a well-qualified human because the robots are much faster and cheaper. Failings of the robots compared to humans will only come to light when a large error is made, e.g., Robodebt, but even repeated failures can easily be discounted as “one-offs”. 

There is a potential that the changes will be positive. The models might steer researchers away from the common errors that are repeatedly made by researchers without sufficient statistical training. Human statisticians could then spend their time more productively on more advanced research questions rather than fixing the same old errors.

Things could also get much worse. There’s been a recent explosion in “fast churn” research papers when an open access dataset is mined by AI simply to get a paper. These papers can be created in under an hour, with the AI doing the analysis and writing the paper with little human input. 

What is our value in an AI future? We can hope that many researchers will still want to talk with a well-qualified colleague about their research question and what they want to achieve. Where the stakes are highest, for example the “top” journals and analyses that will impact government funding, key decision makers will hopefully value human statisticians. We need to be prepared for this huge challenge to our field.
